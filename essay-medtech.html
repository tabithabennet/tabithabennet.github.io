Medical Technology × Ethics

The Surgeon, the Algorithm, and the Question of Who's Responsible

AI-assisted surgery is arriving faster than our ethical and legal frameworks can accommodate. The question of accountability is urgent and unresolved.

TopicMedtech & Ethics
Read time8 min read
ByTabitha Bennet

The Operating Theatre in 2027

Imagine a surgical robot that has performed, in simulation, the equivalent of ten million appendectomies. It knows the optimal incision angle for seventeen different anatomical variations. It can detect micro-bleeds that the human eye misses. It has never been tired, never been distracted, never had a difficult morning before walking into theatre.

Now imagine it makes a mistake. Who is responsible?

The Accountability Vacuum

Medical AI is advancing faster than the legal and ethical infrastructure designed to govern it. In most jurisdictions, liability in medical AI scenarios remains profoundly unclear. If a diagnostic algorithm misclassifies a tumour, is the hospital liable? The software company? The clinician who accepted the recommendation without independent review? The regulator who approved the device?

We are deploying systems that make consequential medical decisions while the frameworks for assigning responsibility when those decisions are wrong remain incomplete.

This isn't a hypothetical. AI diagnostic tools are already in clinical use. Robotic surgical systems are performing procedures in hospitals worldwide. The accountability question is not coming - it's here.

Explainability as a Design Requirement

One of the central problems is that the most capable medical AI models are also the least interpretable. Deep learning systems that outperform radiologists at detecting certain cancers cannot explain, in terms a clinician can evaluate, why they reached their conclusion.

This creates a structural problem for trust. A clinician who overrides an AI recommendation they can't evaluate has no rational basis for the override. A clinician who defers to it without understanding it has abdicated their professional judgement. Neither is acceptable.

Explainability is therefore not just a technical nice-to-have - it's a precondition for responsible deployment. And it's a design requirement, because the explanation needs to be intelligible to a time-pressured clinician, not just a machine learning engineer.

What I Think Needs to Happen

The medtech industry needs to invest as heavily in the human interface layer as in the algorithmic core. Clinicians need tools that show their reasoning, flag their confidence levels, and communicate their limitations. Patients need informed consent frameworks that actually explain what AI involvement in their care means.

Regulators need to move faster and think more carefully about process liability versus outcome liability. And ethicists need to be in the room from the beginning, not called in when something goes wrong.

The technology is extraordinary. The governance is not keeping pace. That gap is where harm lives.
