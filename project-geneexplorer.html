Biotech - Data Visualisation - Harvard 2024

GeneExplorer

An interactive genomic data visualisation platform designed to make complex biotech data legible to non-specialists - built with D3.js and a complete design system.

RoleDesigner & Developer
Timeline2024 - Harvard
StackD3.js, SPA, Design System
ContextMLM Digital Media Design

The Problem

Genomic data is inherently complex - dense, multi-layered, and produced in formats designed for researchers, not patients or clinicians who need fast, actionable insight. Most visualisation tools in the biotech space either oversimplify to the point of meaninglessness, or present raw data with no navigational affordances.

GeneExplorer was built to occupy the gap: sophisticated enough to be genuinely useful, designed carefully enough to be navigable by someone without a genomics background.

The goal wasn't to make genomics easy. It was to make it navigable - a meaningful distinction when the stakes are clinical.

What I Built

The platform is a single-page application built on D3.js, with a complete design system developed in parallel to ensure visual consistency across all data states and interactive elements.

  • Interactive chromosome map with drill-down capability to gene-level detail
  • Variant annotation panel with plain-language explanations alongside clinical notation
  • Comparative view for multi-sample analysis across family or cohort datasets
  • Colour system designed for accessibility - legible under low vision and colour blindness conditions
  • Full design system: tokens, components, states, data visualisation patterns
  • Responsive layout supporting tablet and large desktop screen sizes

Design Decisions

The most contentious design decision was how much clinical information to surface by default. Too little and the tool becomes patronising; too much and the interface collapses under cognitive load. The solution was progressive disclosure - a clean top-level view that invites curiosity, with depth available on demand.

Typography was chosen to reinforce credibility without clinical coldness. Data labels use a monospaced system font for precision; contextual annotations use a warmer serif to signal that interpretation, not just data, is being offered.

What I Learned

Building for expert data is a particular kind of design challenge. Your users know more about the subject matter than you do, which means every simplification risks condescension. The discipline was in learning enough genomics to understand what mattered - and then designing for legibility without losing fidelity.

D3.js is extraordinarily powerful and extraordinarily unforgiving. The project deepened my understanding of data binding, scales, and the performance implications of rendering large genomic datasets in the browser.
