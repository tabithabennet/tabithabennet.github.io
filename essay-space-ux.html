Living Off-Planet: The Invisible UX Problem of Space Habitation

We've spent decades engineering rockets. We've barely started engineering the daily human experience of living inside one.

TopicSpace & Design
Read time8 min read
ByTabitha Bennet

The Rocket Problem Is Solved

Getting to orbit is hard. Staying there for six months without losing your mind, your muscle mass, or your ability to make good decisions under stress - that's the problem nobody is talking about loudly enough.

The space industry is obsessed with propulsion, materials, and orbital mechanics. These are solvable engineering problems with clear metrics. But the human factors - the experience of living in a tube for months, making critical decisions in a cognitively degraded state, interfacing with complex systems at 3am - these are design problems. And they're largely unsolved.

What Cognitive Load Looks Like in Microgravity

On Earth, we design for a user who is tired, distracted, possibly stressed. We talk about reducing cognitive load as though it's a nice-to-have. In space, cognitive load is life-safety critical. An astronaut who misreads an interface during a pressure emergency is not just inconvenienced - they're in danger.

Every interface decision that seems minor on Earth becomes load-bearing in orbit. Font size. Alert hierarchy. The physical placement of a button.

And yet most spacecraft interfaces look like they were designed in the 1990s, because many of them were. Legacy systems persist in aerospace with a tenacity that would be laughable in consumer software.

The Circadian Problem

Human biology runs on light cycles. In low Earth orbit, you see sixteen sunrises per day. The International Space Station keeps artificial day/night cycles, but they're imperfect - and the science on long-duration sleep quality in space is concerning. Sleep deprivation compounds every other cognitive risk.

This is a design problem. Lighting systems, scheduling interfaces, the visual rhythm of a habitat - these directly affect the biological clock of every person inside. We know how to design environments that support circadian health on Earth. We haven't translated that knowledge seriously enough to space habitation.

The Coming Wave

SpaceX, Blue Origin, Axiom - commercial space is accelerating. The next decade will see more humans spending more time in orbit and eventually on the lunar surface than any previous period in history. The people designing those habitats and interfaces have an enormous responsibility.

I'm hopeful, because the best spatial designers and interaction designers in the world are now paying attention to this problem. But we need to move faster. The hardware is ready. The human-centred design is not.
