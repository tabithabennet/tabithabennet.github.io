Otto
A Personal Admin Assistant

An LLM-powered assistant that handles the administrative overhead of running a freelance practice, triaging my inbox, drafting client replies in my own voice, and keeping invoices and deadlines from slipping through the cracks.

RoleDesigner & Developer
Year2025
DomainAI / Agents
StackPython · LLM tool-calling · Email & Calendar APIs

The Problem

Running a freelance practice means living inside your inbox. Between active client threads, invoices to chase, project deadlines, and the constant low hum of scheduling, the administrative load quietly eats the hours that should go into actually building things. None of it is hard, but all of it is relentless, and it is exactly the kind of work that drains focus without ever feeling finished.

Otto started as a simple question: how much of that overhead could an LLM agent take off my plate if it actually understood the context of my work and could act on my behalf?

What Otto Does

  • Triages the inbox. Sorts incoming mail into what needs a reply, what needs scheduling, what relates to money, and what can simply be archived, so the first pass through email takes seconds rather than half an hour.
  • Drafts replies in my voice. Generates ready-to-edit responses grounded in examples of how I actually write, so the drafts sound like me rather than like a template.
  • Tracks invoices and deadlines. Keeps a running view of what has been billed, what is outstanding, and what is due across projects, and flags the things that are about to slip.
  • Acts through tools. Rather than just chatting, Otto can read and draft over email and reason about the calendar, turning a request into an actual action.

The goal was never to remove myself from client relationships, it was to remove the friction between deciding what to say and getting it said.

Why Build It

Beyond the practical time saved, Otto was a way to work hands-on with the questions I find most interesting: how you give a language model reliable access to tools, how you keep an agent grounded in real context rather than letting it improvise, and how you design the human handoff so the person stays in control of anything that actually gets sent.

The Loop

Otto runs as a tool-calling agent. Incoming context is gathered, the model decides what kind of attention each item needs, and any action that leaves a draft or touches the calendar is surfaced for me to approve before anything is sent.

Sounding Like Me, Not Like a Bot

The hardest part of a useful drafting assistant is voice. A reply that is technically correct but tonally off is often slower to fix than writing from scratch. Otto conditions its drafts on a set of my own past correspondence, so it picks up the cadence, the level of formality, and the small habits that make an email read as mine. Every draft lands in a review step, nothing is sent without me.

Keeping the Human in the Loop

Every action that has consequences, a sent email, a confirmed meeting, a chase note, is proposed rather than executed. Otto does the gathering, the sorting, and the drafting; I make the call on anything that leaves my outbox. That boundary is deliberate, and it is what makes the assistant something I actually trust to run against a live inbox.

What's Next

The near-term focus is hardening the invoice and calendar modules into something I rely on without checking, and tightening the review experience so approving a batch of drafts is a single, fast pass. The voice interface is the experiment I am most curious about, taking the same agent and making it something I can talk to while my hands are busy.
