Space Technology × Human Factors

What the New Space Race Gets Wrong About Long-Duration Human Systems

SpaceX and Blue Origin are racing to orbit - but the human workflows, health monitoring, and interfaces for long-duration missions remain embarrassingly primitive.

TopicSpace Systems
Read time7 min read
ByTabitha Bennet

The Infrastructure Obsession

The new space race is captivating. Reusable rockets. Lunar landers. Mars mission timelines that, for the first time in decades, feel like they might actually happen. The engineering achievements of the last five years have been genuinely extraordinary.

But watch how the conversation goes when you ask about the human side of long-duration spaceflight. The answers get vaguer. The timelines get fuzzier. The word "research" appears a lot.

The Human Systems Gap

The average commercial spacecraft interior has received a fraction of the design investment of the average commercial aircraft cabin. This is remarkable given that an astronaut is going to live in that spacecraft for months, not hours.

The consequences are not trivial. Cognitive performance degrades measurably on long-duration missions. Social dynamics in small crews under sustained stress are predictably difficult. The monitoring systems for early detection of health issues are less sophisticated than consumer wearables.

We have solved for getting humans to space. We have not seriously solved for keeping them functional, healthy, and cognitively intact once they're there.

Interfaces at the Edge

Mission-critical interfaces deserve a separate conversation. The complexity of modern spacecraft systems means that astronauts are constantly interfacing with software - monitoring, troubleshooting, executing procedures. Many of these interfaces were designed decades ago and haven't been fundamentally rethought.

Commercial crew vehicles have made some progress here. SpaceX's Dragon interface is touchscreen-based and more modern than the Space Shuttle's instrumentation. But modern doesn't mean well-designed for the specific cognitive demands of orbital operations. There's an enormous amount of interaction design work that hasn't been done.

What Good Looks Like

The best thinking on long-duration human factors in space is coming from an unusual combination of sources: NASA's human research programme, Antarctic and submarine research (the closest Earth analogues), and a small number of design researchers who have started paying serious attention.

Good long-duration habitat design is adaptive, privacy-enabling, and biophilic where possible. Good monitoring systems are continuous, unobtrusive, and interpretable by non-specialists. Good interfaces reduce cognitive load at the moment of highest stress, not when everything is going well.

The companies that take this seriously will have a significant competitive advantage - and more importantly, will send home crews that are whole. That's worth designing for.
