Reading the Genome Like a Story: Why Biotech Needs Narrative Design

The genome is the most complex document ever written. We've learned to read it. We haven't learned to explain it.

TopicBiotech
Read time7 min read
ByTabitha Bennet

The Most Important Text Nobody Can Read

The human genome contains approximately three billion base pairs - four letters, arranged in sequences that encode everything from your eye colour to your predisposition to late-onset Alzheimer's. It took thirteen years and three billion dollars to sequence the first one. You can now get yours done for under a hundred pounds and receive results in six weeks.

And then what? Most people receive a PDF, some charts, and a list of variants with clinical significance ratings they have no framework to interpret. The data arrives. The meaning doesn't.

Science as Story

The history of science communication is a history of failed translations. Scientists are trained to be precise, hedged, and comprehensive. These are virtues in research. They are obstacles in communication. When a genomics report says "you carry one copy of the APOE ε4 allele, associated with increased Alzheimer's risk in some populations," most people hear either "I'm going to get Alzheimer's" or nothing at all.

Narrative is not a simplification of science. It's the technology by which science becomes actionable for real human beings.

The best science communicators - and the best health platforms - understand that humans process meaning through story. Causality. Character. Stakes. Before and after. These are not dumbed-down alternatives to data. They're the structures through which data becomes decision.

What This Means for Biotech Design

I've been building in this space with VITL, and the design challenge is genuinely hard. You need to honour scientific complexity without drowning the user in it. You need to personalise information without catastrophising. You need to communicate risk without inducing paralysis.

The companies getting this right are thinking about information architecture as narrative architecture. What does this person need to understand first? What context makes this finding meaningful? What action does this information enable?

The Stakes

Genomic information is going to become a standard part of healthcare. CRISPR therapies are moving from laboratory to clinic. The decisions people make based on their genomic data - about family planning, preventive treatment, lifestyle intervention - are among the most consequential they'll ever face.

The design of genomic interfaces is therefore one of the highest-stakes design problems in existence. We are building the tools through which people will understand their own heredity. That deserves more than a PDF.
