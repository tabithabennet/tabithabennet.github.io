Healthtech × Personal Data

Your Body Is Generating Data You Can't Read Yet

The biosensors exist. The genomic sequencing exists. The gap is interpretability - and that's fundamentally a design challenge.

TopicHealthtech
Read time6 min read
ByTabitha Bennet

The Data Deluge

Right now, your Apple Watch knows more about your cardiac patterns than most doctors will ever learn from a single appointment. Your gut microbiome contains roughly 38 trillion microorganisms, each generating signals that influence your mood, immune response, and metabolic rate. Your genome holds predispositions to conditions that won't manifest for decades.

None of this is secret. The technology to collect it exists, is affordable, and is increasingly mainstream. The problem is that almost none of it is interpretable by the person it belongs to.

The Interpretability Gap

I've been thinking about this a lot while building VITL Holistic. The single greatest challenge in health optimisation isn't access to testing - it's access to meaning. A VO2 max score is a number. A HRV trend is a graph. A gut microbiome report is a PDF with 40 pages of Latin species names.

Data without narrative is noise. The job of health technology design is to turn biological signal into human meaning.

This is where healthtech consistently fails. Companies invest enormous resources in the science of measurement and almost nothing in the design of interpretation. The result is platforms that technically work and practically confuse.

What Good Design Looks Like Here

The best examples of health data design do a few things well. They prioritise signal over completeness - showing you the three things that matter most, not all 200 biomarkers. They contextualise readings against your own baseline rather than population averages. They translate clinical language into plain English without losing scientific accuracy.

They also respect the emotional weight of health information. Learning something unexpected about your own body is not a neutral cognitive event. The design needs to hold that.

The Personalised Future

The direction of travel is clear: medicine will become increasingly predictive, preventive, and personalised. Nutrigenomics will tell you which dietary interventions work for your specific genome. Continuous glucose monitoring will replace annual blood panels. AI-powered pattern recognition will catch disease signals years before symptoms appear.

All of this will only work if the interfaces through which people encounter their own health data are genuinely good. This is one of the most important design problems of the next decade, and it's barely being treated as a design problem at all.
