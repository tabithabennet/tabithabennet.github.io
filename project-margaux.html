Margaux
An AI Talent Manager

An agent that does the day-to-day work of an influencer manager, sourcing the right brand deals, drafting pitches and replies, and running the deal pipeline, while leaving every decision that touches a creator's reputation or money to a human.

RoleDesigner & Developer
Year2025
DomainAI / Agents
StackPython · LLM tool-calling · Email APIs

The Problem

Talented creators are often badly served by management. The good managers are expensive and selective; the rest can be slow, generic, or quietly working against the creator's interests. A lot of the actual job is structured and repetitive, matching a creator to the right brief, writing a sharp pitch, chasing a quiet thread, keeping a deal moving, which is exactly the kind of work an agent can carry, if it is built with judgement and the right guardrails.

Margaux came out of watching friends who are influencers struggle to find management that was both affordable and actually on their side.

What Margaux Does

  • Sources jobs. Scores every creator against each opportunity on niche, platform, audience, and budget-versus-rate, and explains why each is a fit so the suggestion can be defended.
  • Pitches brands. Drafts a tailored outreach email putting the right creator forward, in a manager's voice.
  • Triages and answers the inbox. Sorts brand enquiries, live negotiations, admin, and low-fit approaches, and drafts replies that protect the creator's rates.
  • Runs the pipeline. Tracks every deal from sourced to paid and flags what has gone quiet, a pitch with no reply, a stalled negotiation, an unpaid invoice.
  • Protects the roster. Knows each creator's floor and the sectors they avoid, and won't put them forward for a deal priced below their worth.

A manager's real job is to protect a creator's relationships and reputation. An agent that can email brands on its own is a liability, not a feature.

The Principle: Prepare, Don't Send

Everything Margaux produces, every pitch, every reply, every negotiation move, is drafted and queued for a human to approve. Real sending exists, but it is off by default and gated behind two deliberate steps. That boundary is not a limitation I worked around; it is the core of the design. The agent does the sourcing, scoring, and writing; the person makes every call that leaves the building.

The Loop

Margaux pulls together three streams, the roster, incoming mail, and the open opportunities, and turns them into a single daily brief: what to reply to, who to pitch where, and which deals need chasing. Claude handles the judgement (reading a negotiation's tone, writing in voice); the scoring that decides who fits a brief is deterministic and fully explainable.

Explainable Matching

A manager should never put a creator forward without being able to say why. Margaux scores each creator against a brief on niche overlap, platform presence, audience reach, and crucially budget against the creator's own rate card, then surfaces the reasons alongside the score. A brief priced well below a creator's floor is pushed down the list, not pitched. An example of the matcher running against a real brief:

The Approval Gate

Outreach is the one place anything leaves the building, and it is deliberately the most constrained part of the system. By default it only ever creates drafts. Sending requires an explicit opt-in in code and a separate email permission, two conscious steps, each logged. The test suite includes a check that sending is blocked unless it has been deliberately enabled, so the safe default cannot quietly regress.

What's Next

The near-term work is hardening the Gmail connector for real day-to-day use and tightening the approval experience so a manager can review a batch of drafts in one fast pass. The larger ambition is auto-sourcing, surfacing relevant briefs from the open web so opportunities arrive without being entered by hand, which is as much a question of doing it responsibly as doing it at all.
